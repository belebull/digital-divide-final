Visualizing the Digital Divide

In a modern world, who has access to internet in the United States?

By Beleicia Bullock

December 2021

In a country that laudes digital innovation as the future, it can be easy to forget that the internet is still a luxury for many communities across the United States. Issues with access to the internet stem from both the systematic exclusion of low-income and racially-diverse communities and infrastructural challenges. Combined together these issues create the foundation for what is known as the digital divide, or the gap in access - and as this article will argue - usage of broadband internet.

In order to first contextualize this problem, we need to understand exactly what broadband internet is. According to Investopedia.com, “broadband refers to various high-capacity transmission technologies used to transmit data, voice, and video across long distances and at high speeds”, including “coaxial cable, fiber optic cable, and radio waves”. First, note the need for “high-capacity” mediums. This means that connecting to broadband requires infrastructure well beyond the cost of the individual consumers. As such, consumers rely on internet service providers to build new infrastructure and offer fair prices to access the internet.

Secondly, the definition also notes that your connection should be fast. In fact, the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) dictates that broadband internet must meet download speeds of 25 megabits per second and upload speeds of 3 megabits per second to qualify broadband. If you think these speeds sound slower than the ones you hear advertised by ISPs on television, you’re not wrong. As Verizon notes, “25 Mbps should be enough” for everyday tasks, but you still might run into lagging and buffering. And definitely don’t expect to be able to play high-bandwidth games!

Despite the seemingly low benchmark for broadband service, it’s eye-opening how many counties and communities across the country may not even have access to that. This article combines broadband data collected by Microsoft with data from the 2017 American Community Survey (ACS) to explore the availability and usage of broadband internet service across U.S. counties. Let's take a broad look at the condition of the country and then dive into the roles of location, class, and race in the digital divide.

Availability and Usage Across the United States

While the digital divide is typically thought of as not having any internet at all, the Harvard Business Review reminds us that this is not simply a task of adding more infrastructure to more rural areas. Efforts are being made in this area to help bolster the wires and structures needed to make sure that children on farms in Alabama can do their homework just as easily in New York City. In fact the map above, provides potentially promising news. At the end of 2017, at least 90% of the population in 1,263 counties had acess to broaband service at speeds defined by the FCC. That's a whopping 40% of counties in the dataset. If you set the threshold a little lower, you'll see that the numbers do even better. In 2,660 counties (or 84% of the counties in the dataset), at least half of the county's population has access to broaband internet.

However, if you look at usage, you get a completely different story. Only 15% of the counties had at least half of their population using broadband internet at the end of 2019 and there are only 5 counties where 100% of the population uses broaband. What's the disconnect between availability and usage - and who is it affecting the most?

Location, Income, and Race - Oh My!

When you sepearte the counties by population or income, you notice some major, if unsurprising, differences. When it comes to location, metro areas, or counties that have more than 50,000 people as per the Health Resources and Services Administration, have higher availability and usage than rural communities. Here, rural simply means anything that is not a metro area, per the Census' definition. Rural, can further be broken up into two micro areas (or those with 2,500 to 49,999 people) and neither metro or micro areas (which has less than 2,500 people).

While it may be easy to dismiss the digital divide issues in these areas due to the "lack of people", it's important to remember how essential the internet is to people, especially in a pandemic. Without reliable internet, people risked not being able to work, get vaccine shots, visit doctors, talk to family or go to school over the last two years alone.

Furthermore, income also plays a role in the availability and usage of broaband within communities. We took the median income of each county and compared it to the national median income in 2017 - $61,372. Using Brookings' defintion of the middle class, we create buckets for low-income communities (those counties whose median income was below 30% of the national median), middle-income communities (counties in which the median was at least 30% of median income, but less than 30% above it), and high-income communities (counties where the median income is more than 30% above the national median). Not only are there far fewer high-income communities, but they also higher levels of populations who can access and use broabdabnd internet. In comparison, the average availability and usage is much lower for middle-income and low-income counties.

So, Where Do We Go From Here?

While you're probably not going to be able to rush out build new infrastructure (even though the Biden administration has rolled out some introductory plans to close this gap), this article does encourage us to think about the digital divide differently. As the the Harvard Review notes, infrastructure is one part of the equation. Why do people under-utilize the internet? How can we make connectivity easier for a variety of communities, especially when considering education, age, ability and yes, location? The divide is more than just getting on to the internet - it's about creating digital spaces that are available to all of us.
